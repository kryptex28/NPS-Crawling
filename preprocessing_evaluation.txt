================================================================================
EVALUATION OF THE PREPROCESSING STAGE
================================================================================


1. WHY WE PREPROCESS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The project's goal is to classify SEC EDGAR filings by how they reference the
Net Promoter Score (NPS). Finding candidate passages relies on a keyword search,
which is high recall but low precision: the keywords also occur in boilerplate,
tables, and unrelated contexts. Preprocessing therefore acts as a semantic
relevance filter that drops the irrelevant hits before the expensive classifier,
keeping the accepted set small and clean.


2. HOW WE PREPROCESS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Around every keyword hit the pipeline extracts a short context window, embeds it
with a sentence-transformer model, and measures its cosine similarity to a fixed
reference text describing an NPS-relevant passage. Windows scoring above a
threshold are accepted; the rest are rejected. The filter's quality thus depends
on two choices - the embedding model and the reference text - and the evaluation
below sweeps both.


3. HOW THE PREPROCESSING WAS EVALUATED
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

We froze a labelling set of 621 context windows that the pipeline actually
produced, and labelled each one by hand as relevant (positive) or not relevant
(negative). The set contains 126 positives and 495 negatives, i.e. roughly a
20% positive rate. Because of this class imbalance, accuracy is misleading and
we report precision, recall, and their harmonic mean, the F1 score, instead.

For each combination of embedding model and reference text, every one of the 621
windows is re-embedded, scored by cosine similarity against the reference text,
and then thresholded. We sweep the acceptance threshold from 0.00 to 0.80 in
steps of 0.02 and, for each (model, reference text) pair, report the best F1
reached over that sweep together with the threshold at which it occurs. We also
record the steady-state embedding time per context window (ms/ctx, measured on
the same hardware and excluding the one-time model load) so that quality can be
weighed against cost.

Three things stand out.

Best cells. The two best configurations of the whole grid are tied at F1 = 0.917:
all-MiniLM-L6-v2 with the V3 keyword anchor, and bge-base-en-v1.5 with the V6
minimal definition. No other configuration beats either of them.

Reference text matters more than model. Moving along a row (changing the
reference text) shifts F1 by far more than moving down a column (changing the
model). The reference text is the semantic anchor: its embedding defines the
direction in vector space that "relevant" points toward, so a poorly chosen
anchor hurts every model. V4 (the narrow executive-compensation / KPI register)
is the weakest column everywhere (0.638-0.807): it is too specific, so it pulls
the anchor away from the general notion of "this passage is about NPS" and
collapses recall. The broad, well-formed definitions (V1, V2, V6) and the
keyword bag (V3) are the strong anchors.

Different models prefer different anchors. This is the most interesting pattern.
all-MiniLM-L6-v2 and all-mpnet-base-v2 peak on V3 (the ungrammatical keyword
bag): 0.917 and 0.863. The BGE models do the opposite - they are weak on V3
(0.826 / 0.819 / 0.870) and peak on V6, a clean grammatical definition sentence
(0.917 / 0.858 / 0.867). The reason is how the models were trained. The BGE
family is trained as retrieval models on a query->passage objective with
natural-language queries; a well-formed sentence like V6 is close to their
training distribution, whereas a grammar-free pile of keywords is not. The
sentence-transformers MiniLM / MPNet models are general sentence-similarity
encoders and are robust to (indeed, here favour) a dense keyword anchor that
front-loads the discriminative vocabulary. In short, you must match the *style*
of the reference text to the family of the model, not just pick "the best model".

Size and speed. Reading the ms/ctx labels top to bottom shows that cost grows
steeply with model size while quality does not. bge-large (335 M params,
1024-dim) is ~35x slower than all-MiniLM (394.2 vs 11.2 ms/ctx) and yet its best
F1 (0.858) is *lower*. all-mpnet (110 M) costs ~8x and never beats the small
models. The two 384-dim models (all-MiniLM at 11.2 ms/ctx, bge-small at
27.0 ms/ctx) are both the cheapest and among the most accurate. On this task the
extra parameters of the large models are not buying relevance discrimination -
they are buying latency.


5. SCORE SEPARATION OF THE TWO BEST MODELS (score_separation_top_models.png)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

This figure takes the two F1-tied top configurations from the heatmap and plots,
for each, the distribution of cosine similarities for the 495 negative windows
(red) against the 126 positive windows (green), with the best threshold marked.

  Left  - bge-base-en-v1.5, V6 definition: F1 = 0.917, P = 0.91, R = 0.92,
          best threshold = 0.52.
  Right - all-MiniLM-L6-v2, V3 keywords:  F1 = 0.917, P = 0.96, R = 0.87,
          best threshold = 0.34.

The two models reach the *same* F1 but get there with very differently shaped
distributions, and this is the key to choosing between them.

bge-base produces a narrow, high-sitting distribution. Its negatives are not
spread out near zero; instead they pile up sharply in a tall band around
0.40-0.48, and the positives sit just above, around 0.55-0.75. The whole used
range is compressed roughly into 0.35-0.85. This is the well-known anisotropy of
BGE-style embeddings: the vectors occupy a narrow cone of the space, so the
cosine similarity between *any* two pieces of English text is already high and
the dynamic range is squeezed into a narrow high band. Even an unrelated
negative therefore scores ~0.4. Because the positive and negative humps sit
close together, the separating threshold has to be pushed up to ~0.52, and it
lands right in the steep flank of the dense negative pile - a small change in the
threshold sweeps a large number of negatives across the boundary, so the
operating point is sensitive.

all-MiniLM produces a wide, low-baseline distribution. Its negatives spread from
~0.0 up to ~0.3 and its positives form a long tail running from ~0.3 out to
~0.75. The embedding space is far less anisotropic, so the cosine score uses much
more of the [0,1] range and the two classes are visually well separated with a
genuine valley between them. The best threshold (0.34) falls in that sparse
valley rather than in the middle of a dense mass, which makes the operating point
robust: there is a comfortable margin in which the threshold can move without
flipping many windows.

Two consequences follow directly:

  - The absolute cosine values are NOT comparable across models. A score of 0.45
    is a strong negative for bge-base but a strong positive for all-MiniLM. The
    threshold is a per-model quantity and must be tuned for each model; it cannot
    be transferred. This is exactly why the optimal threshold for bge-base (0.52)
    is so much higher than for all-MiniLM (0.34) - it is the scale/anisotropy of
    the space talking, not a difference in "how relevant" the windows are.

  - At its best operating point all-MiniLM achieves the higher precision (0.96
    vs 0.91) at slightly lower recall (0.87 vs 0.92). For a relevance *filter*
    feeding a downstream classifier, high precision is the more valuable property:
    it keeps the accepted set clean and avoids passing noise on to the expensive
    classification step.


6. WHY WE CHOSE all-MiniLM-L6-v2
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

all-MiniLM-L6-v2 (with the V3 keyword reference text) was selected for the
production pipeline because it dominates the speed/quality trade-off:

  - Best-in-grid quality. Its 0.917 F1 ties for the single best result of all 30
    configurations; nothing tested is more accurate.

  - Highest precision at that quality. At the chosen operating point it reaches
    P = 0.96, the cleanest accepted set of any top configuration - ideal for a
    pre-classifier filter.

  - Fastest by a wide margin. At 11.2 ms/ctx it is ~2.4x faster than the next
    small model (bge-small, 27.0), ~8x faster than all-mpnet, ~10x faster than
    bge-base, and ~35x faster than bge-large - while matching or beating all of
    them on F1. It is also the smallest model (~22 M params), so it has the
    lowest memory footprint and runs comfortably on CPU, which matters when
    embedding has to scale to the full corpus.

  - More robust threshold. Its wide, well-separated score distribution puts the
    decision boundary in a sparse valley, so the filter is forgiving to small
    threshold drift, unlike bge-base whose boundary sits inside its dense
    negative band.

  - Cheapest, most maintainable anchor. Its best reference text, V3, is just a
    short keyword bag, which is the simplest reference to write and maintain.

In other words, no model beats its F1, and nothing close to its F1 comes close
to its speed; the larger BGE/MPNet models cost up to 35x the compute for equal or
worse relevance. all-MiniLM-L6-v2 sits alone on the favourable corner of the
Pareto frontier - very good and very fast at the same time - which is why it is
the production model. (The deployed pipeline runs it at threshold 0.4, slightly
above the F1-optimal 0.34, deliberately trading a little recall for extra
precision so the accepted set stays clean for the classifier.)
